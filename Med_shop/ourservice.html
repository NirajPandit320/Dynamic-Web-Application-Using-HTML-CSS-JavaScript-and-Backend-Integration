Prescription fulfillment

A prescription that is valid for a pharmacist to fill must include the following information:
Patient information: The patient's name, address, and date of birth.
Clinician information: The clinician's name, address, and DEA number.
Drug information: The drug's name, strength, dosage form, and quantity prescribed.
Date: The date the prescription was issued.
Directions for the pharmacist on how to prepare and dispense the medication, and directions for the patient on how to take the medication

Over-the-counter medications

Active Ingredient: The Substances in the product that cause the intended therapeutic effect. This also states the amount of Substances for each dosage unit.
Purpose: Product action or pharmacologic category.
Uses:Symptoms or diseases the product will treat or prevent.
Warnings: When not to use the product;
Conditions that may require advice from a doctor before taking the product;
Possible interactions or side effects;
When to stop taking the product and when to contact a doctor;
Pregnancy or breastfeeding warnings;
When it is appropriate to talk to a doctor or pharmacist;
Keep product out of childrens reach.
Dosage instructions: How much to take, how to take, how often, and how long to take the drug.
Inactive Ingredients: Substances such as binders, colors or flavorings.
Other Information: How to store the product properly and information about certain ingredients (such as the amount of calcium, potassium, or sodium the product contains).

Vitamins and supplements including multivitamins, omega-3 supplements, and more. Fitness products such as protein powders, energy bars, and weight management supplements. Personal care items such as hair care products, skin care products, and oral care products.

The product is intended only for general wellness use. To meet this criterion, the product would be expected to provide functionality that maintains or encourages a general state of health or a healthy activity.

Consultations with pharmacists

Pharmacist consultations can include:
Medication information: Pharmacists can provide information about a patient's prescribed medications, including:

  • How to take the medication.
  • Why it's being prescribed.
  • How to tell if it's working.
  • Warnings and adverse effects.

    • Interactions : Who to call for questions?
    Medication reviews: Pharmacists can review a patient's medications to ensure they're working effectively and the patient has up-to-date prescriptions. Patients should bring all prescriptions, supplements, and over-the-counter drugs to the consultation.
    Treatment options: Pharmacists can explore available treatment options with patients and discuss the risks and benefits of each.
    Recommendations: Pharmacists can recommend over-the-counter medications for minor health concerns. They can also recommend that a patient speak with a doctor if their symptoms seem concerning or don't improve from over-the-counter drugs.
    A pharmacist consultation might typically include the following stages:
    1. Initiate the consultation: Greet the patient, introduce yourself, and clarify your role.
    2. Gather information: Ask questions about the patient's history and explore their ideas, concerns, and expectations.
    3. Shared decision making: Explore treatment options, discuss risks and benefits, and involve the patient in making decisions.
    4. Close consultation: Confirm that the patient is happy with the plan and set a time for follow-up.
